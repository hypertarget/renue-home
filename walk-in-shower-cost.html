Walk-In Shower Cost in 2026

Most homeowners pay between $3,500 and $18,000 to install a walk-in shower, with tub-to-shower conversions landing closer to $1,500 to $6,000 when existing plumbing lines up. The wide range comes down to four things, scope, materials, labor rates in your area, and how much demo the project requires. This page breaks down what drives the number and what to expect when you talk to a contractor.

What a Walk-In Shower Typically Costs

A basic tub-to-shower conversion, where a contractor removes the tub and installs a prefab shower unit in the existing footprint, runs $1,500 to $4,000 for most homes. The work is largely plumbing, demo, and fitting a fiberglass or acrylic pan and surround. Labor is the biggest cost here, not materials.

Custom tile walk-in showers with a new drain location, bench, rain head, and upgraded fixtures push the budget to $6,000 to $15,000 or more. At that level, you are paying for tile installation, waterproofing membrane, custom glass enclosure, and the extra rough plumbing labor that comes with moving the drain. Primary bathrooms in mid-range homes tend to fall in the $6,000 to $10,000 range when homeowners choose quality tile and frameless glass.

Luxury projects with large-format porcelain tile, niche shelves, steam, and heated floors regularly reach $15,000 to $25,000. That is the upper end, and it is not typical for most renovation budgets.

The Factors That Move the Price

Size matters most at the labor level. A 36-by-36-inch shower takes roughly the same tile time as a 42-by-42-inch shower, but a 5-by-5-foot walk-in with a bench adds meaningful square footage to the tile bid. Each additional square foot of tile typically adds $12 to $25 in labor and materials combined, depending on the tile grade.

Drain relocation is one of the more expensive line items. Moving a drain even 12 inches can require opening the subfloor or, in a slab-on-grade home, cutting concrete. Plumbers charge $500 to $2,000 just for that work, before the tile crew starts. If your current drain lines up with the planned shower footprint, you save real money.

Glass enclosures add $800 to $3,500 depending on whether you choose a framed sliding door, a semi-frameless hinged panel, or a full frameless glass enclosure. Frameless is the most popular choice for resale value but also the most expensive.

Waterproofing membrane is not optional on a properly built shower. Cement board plus a waterproof coating, or a full Schluter system, adds $400 to $900 in materials and labor. Skipping it is the most common reason showers fail within 5 to 10 years.

When to DIY and When to Hire

Tile work is learnable. A patient homeowner comfortable with a wet saw can tile a shower surround and save $800 to $2,500 in labor. The catch is waterproofing. If you get the membrane wrong, you will not know it for two or three years, and by then the framing behind the wall is rotting. Most contractors recommend hiring at minimum the waterproofing and rough plumbing steps, then finishing the tile yourself if you want to save money.

Plumbing and drain work are not good DIY candidates in most jurisdictions. Permits are required, work must be inspected, and shower drain improper pitch causes standing water that feeds mold. The $500 to $1,500 for a licensed plumber to set and slope the drain is worth it.

Glass installation falls in between. Frameless glass panels are heavy and alignment matters. Most homeowners hire that step out and report it goes faster and cleaner than expected.

How to Compare Contractor Bids

Get at least three quotes. More importantly, make sure all three are quoting the same scope. One contractor might include glass. Another might not. One might include a waterproofing membrane as standard, another might list it as an add-on. Ask each bidder to break out materials versus labor so you can compare apples to apples.

Ask specifically what waterproofing system they use. Schluter Kerdi, Wedi, Laticrete Hydro Ban, and RedGard are all accepted systems. A contractor who says they use cement board and that is enough is either inexperienced or cutting a corner.

Check that the contractor pulls a permit for any work that requires one in your jurisdiction. Most bathroom remodels involving plumbing changes require a permit. A contractor who suggests skipping it to save time is taking a risk that becomes yours at resale.

What Affects Regional Pricing

Labor rates for tile work range from $4 to $8 per square foot in rural markets and $10 to $18 per square foot in high-cost metros like San Francisco, New York, or Boston. The same $7,000 shower in Phoenix might cost $11,000 in Seattle. Materials are roughly the same everywhere, so the spread comes almost entirely from labor.

Cities with strong housing markets and low contractor supply, like Denver, Austin, and Nashville, have seen bathroom remodel wait times stretch to 4 to 12 weeks. Factor in schedule when timing a renovation around a household move or rental between occupants.

Related project costs to budget alongside a walk-in shower include bathroom flooring updates and exhaust fan upgrades, which are frequently done at the same time since the space is already open. Bundling keeps mobilization costs down.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a walk-in shower installation take?

A basic conversion with a prefab unit takes 2 to 4 days. A custom tile shower typically takes 5 to 10 working days from demo to final glass installation, depending on how long tile adhesive and grout need to cure before the glass is set. Plan for the bathroom to be out of service for 1 to 2 weeks on a custom build.

Does a walk-in shower add value to a home?

Generally yes, particularly if it replaces a single tub-and-shower combo in a primary bathroom. Remodeling Magazine's Cost vs. Value report consistently shows bathroom remodels recouping 50 to 65 percent of their cost in resale value in most U.S. markets. The return is higher in markets where buyers expect walk-in showers and lower in markets where a bathtub is considered standard.

Can I convert my tub to a walk-in shower without moving the drain?

Often yes. If your tub drain is positioned at the foot of the tub and your new shower footprint sits within the same alcove, most plumbers can adapt the existing drain without cutting into the subfloor. Your contractor will verify during the initial walkthrough. Moving the drain adds $500 to $1,500 in most cases.

What size should a walk-in shower be?

The practical minimum for a comfortable walk-in shower with no door is 36 by 48 inches. A 3-by-3-foot space works but feels cramped for most adults. The sweet spot for comfort and resale is 36 by 60 inches or a corner unit at 42 by 42 inches. Larger master bath projects often go 48 by 72 inches or bigger when space allows.
