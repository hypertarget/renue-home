Walk-In Shower Cost in 2026

Updated June 2026

Walk-in shower costs range from $1,500 to $25,000+ depending on whether you are swapping out a tub or building from scratch, what materials you choose, and where you live. The short version: a basic tub-to-shower conversion runs $1,500 to $4,000, and a custom tile shower with frameless glass lands around $7,000 to $14,000 for most homes (Angi, HomeGuide).

Tub-to-shower conversion

This is the most common starting point. You pull the tub, adapt the drain, and install either a prefab shower unit or a custom tile surround in the same alcove. Prefab acrylic or fiberglass units are the budget choice at $1,500 to $4,000 all in — the materials are inexpensive and installation is fast. Tile surrounds in the same footprint run $3,000 to $6,500, mostly because tile labor takes longer.

The single biggest cost variable in a conversion is whether the drain needs to move. If the existing tub drain lines up with the planned shower drain, you save real money. Moving a drain even 12 inches can require cutting into the subfloor — or, in a slab-on-grade home, cutting concrete. Plumbers typically charge $500 to $2,000 for drain relocation alone, before the tile crew starts (HomeGuide plumbing cost data).

New walk-in shower installation

Building a walk-in shower in a space that never had one is a bigger project. New rough plumbing, a properly pitched drain, waterproofing membrane, and tile all add up. Most of these jobs come in between $4,500 and $9,000 for a standard tile shower with basic glass, and $7,000 to $14,000 when you add frameless glass, a bench, and a rain head (Angi).

Waterproofing is the line item that separates a well-built shower from one that fails in five years. Cement board plus a waterproof coating, or a full Schluter or Wedi system, adds $400 to $900 in materials and labor. It is not optional on a properly built shower, and if a contractor quotes without including it, ask why.

Glass enclosure cost

The glass is often the second-biggest line item after labor. A framed sliding door runs $300 to $800. A semi-frameless hinged panel goes $600 to $1,500. Full frameless glass — the most popular choice for resale — typically adds $800 to $3,500 to the project depending on the number of panels and hardware (This Old House). Frameless installs also take more precision and add labor time.

What else moves the price

  • Tile selection. Ceramic and porcelain tile installed runs about $12 to $25 per square foot for materials and labor combined. Large-format porcelain or natural stone can reach $30 to $60 per square foot.
  • Shower size. Most conversions stay in the existing alcove, so size is set. A larger custom build adds material and labor proportionally.
  • Permit costs. Bathroom remodels involving plumbing changes require permits in most jurisdictions. Budget $250 to $500 for the permit, and verify your contractor pulls it — not having one is a risk at resale.
  • Local labor rates. Tile labor in rural markets runs $4 to $8 per square foot. High-cost metros like Boston, Seattle, or San Francisco can run $10 to $18. The same materials cost roughly the same everywhere; labor is where prices diverge (NKBA market data).

DIY vs. hiring out

Tile work is learnable. A patient homeowner with a wet saw can tile a shower surround and save $800 to $2,500 in labor. The catch is waterproofing — if the membrane is wrong, you will not know for two or three years. Most contractors suggest hiring at minimum the rough plumbing and waterproofing, then finishing tile yourself if you want to save money.

Drain work and plumbing changes are not good DIY projects in most jurisdictions. Permits require inspections, and improper drain pitch causes standing water that feeds mold. The $500 to $1,500 for a licensed plumber to set and slope the drain correctly is worth it.

Is a walk-in shower worth it at resale

In most markets, replacing a tub-shower combo in a primary bathroom with a walk-in shower is a neutral-to-positive move for resale, as long as you keep at least one tub in the home. Buyers in many markets expect walk-in showers in a primary bath. Remodeling Magazine's Cost vs. Value Report puts bathroom remodels at roughly 65 to 70 percent cost recoupment nationally. If there is only one bathroom in the home, keep the tub.
